# Lore Guide — Last Spring

## Premise

**Last Spring** is set after a long technological collapse triggered by orbital war and global electromagnetic devastation.

The old electronic world did not survive. Satellites, grids, networked industry, and most computational infrastructure were destroyed or rendered unreliable. What remained was scavenged, reinterpreted, and rebuilt into a new civilization based on:

- mechanical computation
- low-energy systems
- repairable tools
- biological production loops
- careful, hidden survival

Humanity did not recover by restoring digital civilization. It recovered by becoming slower, quieter, and more mechanical.

---

## Core Setting

There are now two important layers of human existence:

### 1. The Cosmic Station

A remote orbital or extra-terrestrial refuge preserves human continuity, trained operators, archives, and long-term strategy.

This station is not a heroic command center. It is a patient survival apparatus.

In older records and internal station language, it is often referred to simply as **the Ark**.

Its responsibilities include:

- preserving human knowledge
- selecting the next operator to deploy
- monitoring Earth indirectly
- deciding what knowledge matters enough to preserve between runs

The player begins above Earth, not on it.

Each run is framed as the deployment of one more human operator into the degraded terrestrial system.

### 2. Earth

Earth is habitable, but damaged, fragmented, and dangerous.

It is defined by:

- ruined industrial infrastructure
- scavenged shelters
- fungal and algal ecologies
- low-visibility survival
- residual surveillance risk
- incomplete knowledge

Direct human exposure is costly and dangerous. Much of the player’s practical action is therefore displaced onto small analog drones and workshop systems.

Earth also contains dormant human infrastructure in the form of sealed shelters and cryogenic reserves. The surviving system is built around staggered human deployment rather than mass resettlement.

This creates an important social rule of the setting:

- active human presence is limited
- human lives are rationed strategically
- replacement is possible, but never casual
- each deployment is treated as costly and probably temporary

---

## Why The World Became Mechanical

After orbital EMP warfare, electronics became:

- scarce
- unreliable
- hard to repair
- strategically dangerous to depend on

Communities that survived learned to value systems that were:

- visible
- legible
- hand-serviceable
- low-power
- manufacturable from mixed local materials

This pushed civilization toward:

- punch-tape programming
- wind-up and spring-driven machines
- fluid, gear, and belt power transmission
- mechanical instrumentation
- biological support systems

In this world, technology is not absent. It is **recomposed**.

---

## The Invisible Threat

The old war did not only destroy civilization. It left behind an enforcement condition.

Autonomous orbital systems continue to scan for signs of technological resurgence. Whether they are fully functional military remnants, degraded kill-sat networks, or automated strategic doctrine made permanent, the practical result is the same:

- visible technological growth is dangerous
- detectable industry is punished
- survival depends on remaining low-signature

The most dangerous signatures include:

- heat
- CO2 concentration
- noise
- magnetic anomalies

This is why the setting values zero-waste, low-energy, quiet systems. It is not only ecological discipline. It is a survival strategy against detection.

---

## The Shelter

The shelter is the terrestrial operational base.

It functions as:

- workshop
- archive
- launch bay
- repair station
- knowledge cache

The current prototype represents this through the workshop scene:

- programming bench
- cartridge stores
- drone cabinets
- route table

In fiction, this is where the deployed operator studies old programs, produces new cartridges, loads drones, and plans exploration.

The shelter is also the origin point on the route table and larger map.

Some shelters may also function as local cryo banks, machine archives, or relay points between older survival cells.

---

## The Operator

The player is not merely a survivor improvising alone. The player acts through a selected **operator** deployed from the cosmic station.

An operator is defined by:

- training
- memory
- interpretation of incomplete archives
- ability to maintain analog systems
- ability to annotate and preserve knowledge

Later, different operators may shape a run through:

- starting knowledge
- practical biases
- preferred systems
- interpretive notes left in the journal

The operator is important because this game is not only about machines. It is about **who understands the machines and what they record**.

The old design direction also adds a harsher social truth:

- operators are precious, but also expendable
- no single deployment is assumed to succeed
- the system is built to continue after failure

This tension should stay present in the fiction. The operator matters personally, but the survival apparatus is larger than any one person.

---

## Drones

Drones are the player’s main field instruments.

They are not sleek robots or autonomous AI agents. They are:

- analog automata
- modular mechanical tools
- semi-autonomous field instruments
- repairable machine-animals

Current prototype examples:

- a mechanical spider used as an industrial walker / inspection machine
- a wind-up butterfly used as a delicate observation or signaling automaton

Their intelligence is constrained and physical:

- they execute punch-tape programs
- they spend stored mechanical energy
- they expose visible components and readable state
- they fail in understandable ways

Drones extend the operator’s reach into the outside world without requiring full human exposure.

---

## Punch-Tape Programming

Programming in **Last Spring** is a physical act.

Instructions are not typed into an invisible abstract machine. They are:

- selected mechanically
- punched into paper tape
- stored as labeled cartridges
- loaded physically into drones

This matters to the fiction.

Programs are:

- finite
- material
- fragile
- nameable
- preservable

A program is not just code. It is a physical artifact of thought.

The 5-bit punch language reflects this design:

- fixed physical symbols
- readable opcode rows
- numeric argument rows
- compact, learnable mechanical logic

---

## Cartridges

Cartridges are the physical medium of stored machine behavior.

They are important because they combine:

- memory
- identity
- scarcity
- wear

A cartridge can be:

- blank
- programmed
- labeled
- stored
- loaded into a drone
- worn by repeated use
- eventually recycled or destroyed

This gives programming a survival cost. Knowledge is material, not free.

---

## Power and Energy

Energy in this world is mechanical and finite.

In the current prototype, bots operate using installed pre-wound power units rather than abstract fuel bars. This reflects a larger setting rule:

- energy must be prepared
- energy must be installed physically
- energy is consumed through action
- exhaustion can strand machines in the field

Later systems may expand this into:

- rewinding
- manufacturing
- salvage
- biological support loops

But the core rule remains: power is never magical or invisible.

Historically, shelters and workshops may draw from a mix of:

- wound springs
- wind
- water
- animal traction
- biological conversion systems

The prototype currently focuses on compact mechanical power units because they fit the workshop loop best, but the wider world should still feel energetically diverse and improvised.

---

## Biological Survival Systems

Mechanical civilization is only half of survival. The other half is biological maintenance.

Food, oxygen, humidity balance, waste control, and stealth all depend on closed biological loops.

Important systems in the setting include:

- algae reactors for oxygen and food production
- insect farms for protein and waste conversion
- mushroom chambers for decomposition, humidity stability, and supplementary food
- bioluminescent cultures for low-signature lighting

These systems matter for three reasons:

- they keep people alive
- they reduce detectable waste
- they convert byproducts back into useful material

This is why the setting should never feel like pure steampunk. It is **bio-mechanical survival**.

---

## The Outside World

The outside world is not fully known.

It is discovered through:

- route-table observation
- drone movement
- scanning
- repeated expeditions
- persistent notes

Important features of the fiction:

- the shelter is the origin
- discovered objects matter
- routes are learned gradually
- not all knowledge is trustworthy on first encounter

The larger map is not a digital satellite view. It is a mechanical, partial, human-maintained model of the world.

Over longer time scales, the outside world may reveal:

- abandoned industrial zones
- fungal overgrowths
- algae fields
- silent infrastructure remnants
- other shelters
- rare emerging colonies
- low-signal communication traces

---

## The Journal

The journal is the most important long-term progression system in the fiction.

It exists as the operator’s and station’s surviving knowledge body.

It records:

- Earth observations
- discovered locations
- discovered objects
- route history
- programming language understanding
- programs already created
- biological and genetical information
- practical annotations
- manual notes added by the player

The journal is what survives between runs.

This is a key world rule:

- machines may be lost
- cartridges may wear out
- deployments may fail
- but knowledge, once properly recorded, can accumulate

The journal is therefore both:

- archive
- memory
- progression

It also serves a civilizational function. Over time, many journals can become a distributed survival record: a colony network made first of knowledge, and only later of people.

It is the bridge between runs and the main proof that humanity is still learning.

---

## Roguelite Fiction

A run should be understood fictionally as:

- one deployment cycle
- one operator’s practical attempt to extract knowledge and act on Earth
- one material expedition under scarcity

Run failure does not mean total narrative erasure.

Instead:

- material state may be lost or reset
- local field arrangements may collapse
- but preserved knowledge can return to the station/journal layer

This is the intended fiction behind the roguelite structure.

You do not keep everything.
You keep what was understood well enough to record.

This is the world’s answer to human mortality and material fragility:

- bodies fail
- shelters fall
- machines break
- but recorded understanding can still accumulate

---

## Threat and Tension

The world’s tension comes from surviving quietly under conditions of scarcity and incomplete control.

Important tensions include:

- finite physical media
- energy limits
- fragile drones
- environmental unknowns
- possible surveillance or detection
- the cost of visible technological activity
- the expendability of each deployment
- the pressure to turn every failure into preserved knowledge

The mood should remain:

- quiet
- patient
- fragile
- ingenious
- tense

This is not a power fantasy about restoring lost grandeur.
It is about building survivable competence from damaged systems.

---

## Technology Ethics

The setting assumes a cultural shift away from opaque, centralized, disposable technology.

What is valued now:

- repairability
- readability
- modularity
- frugality
- zero-waste thinking
- local control
- biological integration

What is distrusted:

- hidden complexity
- dependency on black-box systems
- irreversible waste
- high-energy signatures

This ethical layer should inform:

- workshop design
- machine design
- UI
- progression
- player choices

---

## Visual Canon

The world should visually read as:

- dark steel and brass
- paper tape and inked records
- workshop instruments
- mechanical displays
- biological support apparatus
- analog gauges and indicators

It should not read as:

- glossy sci-fi
- clean cyberpunk
- digital futurism

The operative visual identity is:

**mechanical survival civilization**

---

## Canon Summary

If a future feature conflicts with this summary, this file should be treated as the default source of truth.

**Last Spring** is about a post-electronic human civilization that survives by deploying trained operators from the Ark into a damaged Earth, where survival depends on quiet mechanical systems, closed biological loops, physical programming media, and persistent journals. Material tools are fragile and finite, human lives are rationed, and orbital detection punishes visible resurgence. Knowledge is the only durable victory.
