# LinkedIn Intro — Last Spring

## Short Pitch

**Last Spring** is a post-collapse survival strategy prototype built in Godot. Humanity survives on a dying orbital refuge called the **Ark**, where only about **500-600 people** remain. At around **40 years old**, each person is given a final choice: become **biomass** for the continuation of the station, or be dropped to Earth as an operator and try to build a hidden shelter.

The game is about that second path.

The player manages a tactile tabletop shelter, programs mechanical drones with punch tape, scavenges quietly, researches what is found, and tries to preserve enough knowledge for the next attempt. The larger hope is fragile: maybe, if enough operators survive long enough and reconnect across generations, humanity can someday find a way to repopulate Earth.

## Genre

- survival strategy
- roguelite
- card-based management
- analog automation / programming
- post-collapse speculative fiction
- stealth-driven systems game

## Core Mechanics

- **Tabletop shelter management**
  The shelter is represented as a physical worktable of cards: machines, drones, tapes, locations, enemies, materials, blueprints, storage, and crafted tools.

- **Punch-tape programming**
  Drones are programmed materially. Instructions are punched into tape, loaded into a machine, and executed in the field.

- **Indirect exploration**
  The player acts through specialized drones rather than solving the world by direct presence alone. The butterfly drone handles passive scouting and scanning, while the spider drone handles scavenging, transport, and combat.

- **Stealth as survival**
  Earth is still watched by persistent orbital machine surveillance. Survival depends on staying low-signature: quiet systems, low-energy infrastructure, limited exposure, and careful growth.

- **Research as progression**
  Knowledge is material. The operator researches locations, materials, enemies, and equipment through a journal. Research can fail, consume resources, and reveal only partial recipes until enough of the world has been understood.

- **Blueprint crafting**
  New tools and structures are unlocked through discovered recipes. Crafting depends on the right blueprint, the right context, and the right physical resources.

- **Bio-mechanical production**
  The shelter economy combines salvage and analog machinery with biological loops: algae, bacteria, mushrooms, mealworms, medicine, rations, and tank-based processing.

## Lore / Setting

In **Last Spring**, humanity did not recover on Earth. After orbital war and electromagnetic collapse destroyed most of the electronic world, the survivors withdrew to the **Ark**, a small orbital refuge that has endured for centuries.

But the Ark is not a thriving civilization. It is a shrinking survival system. Its population is down to only a few hundred people, and every life is counted against the system’s ability to continue.

That creates the central social rule of the setting:

- at around **40 years old**, a person must choose
- remain on the Ark and be converted into **biomass** for the station’s survival
- or descend to Earth as an **operator**

Going to Earth is not a heroic mission in the usual sense. It is a desperate, rationed deployment into a damaged world that is still under orbital surveillance. The operator’s task is to build a hidden shelter, survive quietly, gather knowledge, and create the conditions for others to follow.

This has been happening for **hundreds of years**.
It has had **very little success**.

The dream is still alive, but only barely: if enough operators survive, if enough shelters remain hidden, and if enough fragments of knowledge survive between them, maybe humanity can reconnect the scattered effort and eventually make Earth habitable for people again.

## Core Tension

The game’s long-term tension is not simply survival. It is survival under a civilizational deadline.

- the Ark is finite
- human lives are rationed
- Earth is watched
- operators are expendable
- shelters are temporary
- machines fail
- but recorded knowledge can persist

That makes the journal central to the fiction. The real long-term victory is not keeping one operator alive forever. It is preserving enough understanding that the next operator has a better chance.

## What Makes It Distinct

- programming is treated as a **physical medium**, not an abstract menu
- the player acts through **mechanical drones and partial knowledge**, not direct power fantasy control
- stealth is a **world rule**, not an optional flavor layer
- the economy is built from **paper, salvage, biology, and low-energy systems**
- the game’s larger arc is about **a multi-century attempt to return humanity to Earth**
